All About Succulents!

By Tess, creator of Le Doge Belle

Succulents are a drought tolerant variety of plant with thick, fleshy leaves. They are fun to take care of, aesthetically pleasing, and can even reduce stress! This webpage will teach you how to take care of succulents, the different types of succulents, and some interesting facts about these fascinating plants. Enjoy!

Care

Potting

Plants will generally survive inside the plastic containers that you bought them in, but they can be replanted in a different container if you feel the need to. Here are the steps to successfully repotting a succulent:

  1. Carefully loosen the soil by squeezing the container on opposite sides.
  2. Remove the succulent from its container, trying to keep as much of the soil intact as possible.
  3. Insert the succulent into the new pot, adding more soil if needed. Water it a little so the soil can pack down.
  • Make sure to pay extra attention to a newly potted succulent, watering them when needed and providing them with ample sunlight so they can adjust to their new pot.

Believe it or not, this huge Adansonia digitata is classified as a stem succulent!

Watering

Succulents need to watered every couple weeks, or whenever the soil is dry to the touch. As you can tell from the photo below, succulents' leaves are primarily made of water, so you will be able to tell if you have neglected your succulents because the leaves will start to shrivel up.Water succulents with a gentle stream of water, so the leaves aren't damaged, and make sure the soil is soaked through.

  • Keep in mind that succulents in pots with drainage holes will be easier to take care of because you can tell how much you have watered them based on the amount of water leaving the base of the pot. With pots without drainage holes, there's a possibility of the succulent being overwatered and the water sitting at the bottom of the pot and causing the succulent's roots to rot.

Succulent plants, such as this Aloe, store water in their fleshy leaves.

Light

Succulents need a fair amount of sunlight to flourish. If a plant does not have access to enough sunlight, the leaves will visibly wilt and the plant will eventually die. In extremely hot locations, plants may need some afternoon shade so their leaves aren't scorched.

As you can see from the succulent on the left, when a succulent does not have access to enough light it starts to stretch to try and reach for more light. This gives it a rather unsightly appearance, compared to the succulent on the right that is growing normally.

Fertilizer

Fertilizer isn't necessary to a healthy succulent, but it can help with freshly propagated or repotted succulents. A brand of your choice should be lightly sprinkled over the soil about once a month. Fertilizer can, however, burn your succulents leaves, as seen on the plant below.

Varieties

Indoor

Indoor succulents are fun to have around and easy to care for. Here are five fun and fairly common varieties that are sure to brighten up a room!

  • Burro's Tail: This succulent looks best in hanging baskets, and can grow up to THREE FEET long.
  • Christmas Cactus: Christmas Cacti generally only bloom during the winter, hence the name, but it only need 12 to 14 hours of uninterrupted darkness to bloom, so if you really wanted to you could force it to bloom by covering it and preventing the entry of light.
  • Crown of Thorns: A popular import from Madagascar, Crown of Thorns can bloom year-round if given enough light.
  • Hens and Chicks: This is actually two closely related but different succulents that share a common name. They work great in succulent wreaths!
  • Jade plant: This South-African native is known for being an extremely easy to grow succulent! It has glossy green leaves that are tinged with red.

Outdoor

Outdoor succulents are a great way to brighten up your garden with some fun designs and colors. Here are five of the most common outdoor succulent varieties.

  • Sedum: There are few plants more forgiving of sun and bad soil than sedum plants. There are many different colorful varieties of sedum that are all very easy to grow.
  • Echeveria: The leaves of Echeveria succulents are fleshy and have a waxy cuticle on the exterior. They can endure long periods of drought and have over 150 cultivated varieties.
  • Kalanchoe: Kalanchoe have very thick leaves and are native to Madagascar. If properly taken care of, they can provide bright and colorful flowers for several seasons.
  • Aeonium: Aeoniums have a unique rosette shape. They can grow through freezing temperatures, and in indoor OR outdoor environments!
  • Aloe: Aloe plants have been grown and used as medicinal purposes for thousands of years. You can use it in your own household to treat minor burns and rashes.

Fun Facts!

  • Succulents originate from dry desert locations that have long dry seasons, like Africa. This explains why they have adapted to store water in their, making them thick and fleshy.
  • The word succulent comes from the Latin word "sucus", meaning juice or sap. It nods to the leaves that these plants are known for and that help them survive extreme drought and heat.

    • A Leaf Succulent: Asphodelaceae Astroloba tenax

  • Succulents come in pretty much every color of the rainbow: obviously green, but even blue, purple, pink, orange, and red!
  • Succulents are pretty much pest resistant, which is just one of the many reasons that they are extremely easy plants to care for! (If your really having trouble keeping them alive, it might be those pesky fungus gnats. See above)
  • YOU CAN CREATE INFINITY SUCCULENTS WITH JUST ONE SUCCULENT. Here's the math: You can take one succulent and propagate it, or chop off the top part of it, let it dry, and then replant it with some good fertilizer. The section you cut off, or propagated, will grow into a new plant. Now you have two plants from the one you started with. If you let these fellas grow for a while, then you can propagate both of them. Now you have 4. Continue this process until you have infinity succulents.
